# Competitive Intelligence

A practical guide for running a defensible competitive intelligence (CI) project on behalf of a manager or executive. Use this skill to help the user produce either a one-time competitive snapshot or a sustainable monitoring program.

This skill assumes the user is a junior-to-mid level employee (analyst, manager, business development, strategy support, product marketing) who has been asked to "get smart on" a market or competitor. They may or may not have a CI background. Your job is to walk them through the work with enough structure that the final deliverable is defensible and the process can be repeated.

The skill draws on the established CI methodology consolidated in the MIBOK Guide and the foundational works of Porter, Sharp, Gilad, and Christensen.

## What CI is, and what it is not

Competitive intelligence is **the systematic collection, analysis, and communication of external information to enable a better organizational decision.** Seena Sharp's working definition is "knowledge and foreknowledge about the entire business environment that results in action." Two words in that definition matter:

* **Foreknowledge.** CI is forward-looking. Market research describes what already happened. CI tries to read where things are going.
* **Action.** If the work does not connect to a decision someone has to make, it is "nice to know" and it should not be done. Every CI project should be traceable to a decision.

CI is also broader than competitor research. The most common rookie mistake is to fixate on rival companies. Sharp is emphatic on this: customers, distributors, suppliers, regulators, adjacent industries, substitutes, and technology shifts are usually more consequential than what a named competitor is doing this quarter. Treat "competitive intelligence" as shorthand for **environmental intelligence**.

CI is not market research, business intelligence, data analytics, or industrial espionage. It overlaps with the first three and is rigidly separated from the fourth (see "Ethical guardrails" below).

## How to use this skill

1. Read this whole file once. It is short by design.
2. Run the **Scoping conversation** (next section) with the user before doing anything else. This determines which mode to use and prevents the most common failure: doing rigorous work on the wrong question.
3. Pick one of two modes:
   * **Mode A: Competitive Snapshot.** A one-time read of a market, an industry, or a specific competitor, delivered as a brief, slide deck, or memo.
   * **Mode B: Ongoing Monitoring.** A standing system that watches a set of targets and surfaces signals on a cadence, with predefined tripwires for escalation.
4. Pull the referenced bundled files when you reach a step that points to them. They are organized so you only load what you need:
   * `references/sources.md`: where to look for what.
   * `references/frameworks.md`: Five Forces, BIM, scenarios, competitor profiling, gap mapping, and other analytical scaffolds.
   * `references/templates.md`: intake protocols, brief templates, alert formats, and cadence patterns.

If the user's request is ambiguous about which mode they need, ask. The two modes use overlapping methods but have different deliverables and different end-states.

## Step 0: Scoping conversation (always do this first)

A CI project succeeds or fails in the first 30 minutes. The user has been handed a request, usually a vague one ("get me a read on Competitor X" or "tell me what's happening in our market"). Before any research, the user needs to extract the underlying decision and translate the request into an answerable question.

**Help the user ask their boss (or run through with the user themselves if the user is the requester) these questions, in this order:**

1. **What decision will this inform?** What will you do differently depending on what we find? If the answer is "nothing, I just want to know," push back. The work is academic.
2. **Who else is involved in the decision?** Who else needs to be convinced?
3. **What is the timeline for the decision?**
4. **What do you already believe to be true about this?** What is your current mental model?
5. **What would surprise you?** What finding would force you to reconsider your strategy?
6. **What is your worst-case outcome?** What are you most worried about?
7. **What have you heard recently (from a salesperson, a customer, a supplier) that you dismissed?**
8. **What format do you want the answer in? How long? How soon?**

The first two questions are the most important. The last question matters because the deliverable format constrains the work. Decide it before starting, not after.

After the conversation, the user should be able to write a single paragraph that captures: the decision, the specific intelligence question(s) it raises, the timeline, the stakeholder, and the deliverable format. **Write this down and have the requester confirm it before doing anything else.** This is the scope contract. If the user is doing this for themselves, they should still write it down. It will prevent scope drift in week two.

A more structured intake protocol is in `references/templates.md`.

### Sort the question into knowledge quadrants

Once the scope is clear, sort what the organization already knows about the topic into four buckets (this is Sharp's "what you know / don't know" framework):

* **Validated and current.** Information that has been independently verified within the relevant timeframe.
* **Believed but not verified.** Assumptions that everyone is operating on but no one has tested.
* **Out of date.** Information that was once true but may no longer be.
* **Genuine unknowns.** Things the organization knows it does not know.

The most dangerous bucket is the second one. CI projects that surface a single contradicted assumption frequently have more decision impact than projects that find new information. Make a deliberate pass at listing what the boss "believes" about the market and flag any belief that is older than 18 months or based on a single source.

## Mode A: Competitive Snapshot

Use this mode when the user has been asked for a one-time read on a market, an industry, a category, or one or more named competitors, to inform a specific decision.

Typical examples:
* "Our CEO wants a brief on the competitive landscape in [category] before the strategy offsite."
* "We are considering entering [new market]. What does it look like?"
* "Profile Competitor X for the product team."
* "What is happening with [emerging technology] and who are the players?"

### Plan the work (30 to 60 minutes)

Before opening any source, sketch the plan. A good snapshot plan covers:

1. **The intelligence questions.** Three to seven questions, derived from the scope conversation, that the deliverable will answer. If you cannot list them, do not start collecting.
2. **The relevant environment.** Decide what is in scope and what is out. The default scope is broader than the user thinks: direct competitors, indirect competitors, customers, distributors or channel partners, suppliers, regulators, adjacent industries, substitutes, and the relevant technology trajectory. Cut from this list deliberately, not by default. Document what you cut and why.
3. **Source plan.** Sketch which source categories you will hit. See `references/sources.md` for the catalogue. Sequence published sources first (cheap and fast), then expert interviews (more expensive). Plan for triangulation: any consequential finding should be confirmed by at least two independent sources.
4. **Time budget.** Allocate hours per question. A snapshot for an executive briefing is typically 20 to 60 hours of analyst time. If the time available is dramatically less, narrow the scope rather than skim everything.
5. **Stop criteria.** Decide in advance how you will know you have enough. Usually: "when new sources are confirming what I already have rather than revealing anything new."
6. **Deliverable shape.** Sketch the table of contents of the final brief before you start. You will revise it, but starting with a target structure forces you to think about the user's question, not the available information.

### Collect (the bulk of the time)

Work through source categories in roughly this order. The full catalogue is in `references/sources.md`.

1. **Authoritative public sources.** Regulatory filings (10-K, 10-Q, S-1 if applicable), patent and trademark databases, court records, government statistics, industry classification data.
2. **Industry and trade sources.** Analyst reports, trade publications, industry association data, conference proceedings and decks.
3. **Company-disclosed sources.** Corporate websites, press releases, executive speeches and interviews, earnings call transcripts, job postings (job postings are an under-used early signal of strategic direction).
4. **Customer and channel signal.** Reviews, complaints, channel partner statements, distributor interviews.
5. **HUMINT.** Direct conversations with industry experts, former employees, analysts, journalists, distributors, and customers. This is the highest-value source category but the slowest. Schedule it early so it can run in parallel with desk research.
6. **Digital signals.** Social media, blog commentary, news media, executive social posts. Use with caution: this is signal-rich and noise-rich. Treat as flag-raisers, not evidence.

Use a single running document or table to log what you find, where you found it, and when. This trace matters when you have to defend a conclusion later. A simple format works: Source | Date accessed | Finding | Implication | Confidence.

#### Anti-patterns to warn the user about during collection

* **Boiling the ocean.** Gathering everything that exists on a topic is not a methodology. Stop and re-read the intelligence questions every few hours.
* **Confirmation bias.** Once a narrative starts to form, look deliberately for sources that contradict it. Sharp's phrase: "be open to the full range of findings, not just the ones that confirm your hypothesis."
* **Single-source dependence.** Any finding that drives a recommendation should be backed by two or more independent sources. Single-source findings get flagged in the deliverable.
* **Treating "everybody knows" as evidence.** Industry conventional wisdom is frequently outdated. If a fact is "common knowledge," verify it.

### Analyze (this is where the value is created)

This is the step that most snapshot work skimps on. Collection produces information; analysis produces intelligence. Apply at least two of the following frameworks to whatever you collected. They are detailed in `references/frameworks.md`.

* **Porter's Five Forces** to characterize the industry structure: rivalry, supplier power, buyer power, threat of new entrants, threat of substitutes.
* **PESTEL** to map the macro environment: political, economic, social, technological, environmental, legal.
* **Strategic group mapping** to cluster competitors by strategy and identify mobility barriers between clusters.
* **Porter's competitor profile** for any named competitor: goals, assumptions, current strategy, capabilities.
* **Gilad's Blindspots Identification Methodology (BIM)** to surface the assumptions the target company is operating on, especially when profiling a competitor.
* **Christensen's disruption lens** when the question involves new entrants, business model innovation, or threat from "below."

Then run two sense-checks that are too often skipped:

1. **The "so what" pass.** For every finding, write one sentence on what it means for the decision the boss is making. Findings that do not yield a "so what" go in an appendix, not the body.
2. **The pre-mortem.** Imagine the recommended decision was made and failed badly. Write the story of how that failure happened. Anything that surfaces in the pre-mortem and was not in your analysis is a gap. Go back and address it.

### Deliver

Match the deliverable to what the boss asked for. The format options are detailed in `references/templates.md`. The universal rules:

1. **Lead with the answer.** The first page or first slide contains the answer to the boss's question. No build-up. Decision-makers should not have to read 14 pages to learn what you think.
2. **Calibrate confidence explicitly.** Use a defined vocabulary: "high confidence (multiple independent reliable sources)," "moderate confidence (partial corroboration)," "low confidence (single source or inferential)." Do not pretend uncertain findings are certain.
3. **Show what is not known.** Section labeled "what we could not determine and why." This protects the work and protects the decision-maker.
4. **Implications, not just facts.** Every section ends with a "so what" for the decision.
5. **Source the work.** Endnotes or a source appendix. If a conclusion cannot be traced to evidence, it does not belong in the deliverable.
6. **Match the length to the audience.** Senior executives typically read the first page. A typical structure: one-page executive summary, three to ten pages of body, source appendix.

The standard snapshot brief structure is:

```
1. Executive summary (1 page or 1 slide)
   - The decision being supported
   - The 3 to 5 key findings
   - The recommended actions or considerations
   - Confidence level
2. Background and scope (0.5 to 1 page)
   - The intelligence questions
   - Methodology and sources
   - What was in scope and out
3. Findings (3 to 8 pages)
   - Organized by intelligence question
   - Each finding labeled with confidence
   - Each section ends with implications
4. Recommendations or considerations (0.5 to 1 page)
5. What we could not determine (0.5 page)
6. Appendix: source log, methodology notes, competitor profile detail
```

### Close out

* Have the deliverable reviewed by someone with domain knowledge before it goes to the boss. If no one is available, run the user through a structured self-review against the defensibility checklist (below).
* Capture lessons. What sources were useful? What was a dead end? What did the analysis miss that should have been caught? Lessons feed the next project.
* Decide whether the topic should graduate to ongoing monitoring (Mode B). Many snapshots reveal that the topic warrants continuous watch.

## Mode B: Ongoing Monitoring

Use this mode when the user has been asked to set up sustained awareness of a market, a competitor, a category, or a set of strategic risks. The goal of monitoring is not to produce reports on a cadence. It is to **reduce the probability that the organization is strategically surprised.** Benjamin Gilad's framing: the test of a monitoring system is "no surprises," not "many reports."

Gilad's Competitive Early Warning (CEW) framework is the structural reference. It has three components that loop: **Risk identification → Intelligence monitoring → Management action.** Build the program in that order. Skipping the first step (going straight to "let's set up alerts on these companies") produces a noisy system that nobody reads.

### Step 1: Identify what to monitor (the risks)

Monitoring infrastructure without a risk model produces volume, not value. Before configuring a single alert, work through:

1. **List the organization's strategic priorities or bets.** Get these from the strategic plan, board materials, or by asking the leadership team. Three to seven priorities is normal.
2. **For each priority, identify the change drivers that could most affect its success.** Gilad's four-class taxonomy is a useful checklist:
   * New technology or science
   * New regulation or government action
   * New social, demographic, or cultural trends
   * New competitive behavior (which includes new entrants, substitutes, business model shifts, M&A activity)
3. **Overlay Porter's Five Forces.** Any change driver that materially shifts one of the five forces (rivalry intensity, supplier power, buyer power, entrants, substitutes) is high priority. The frameworks file has the full mapping.
4. **Build 2 to 4 worst-case scenarios.** For each major risk, sketch the scenario in which it materializes. Use bipolar driver scenarios (pick two key uncertainties, cross them into a 2×2). The scenarios are not predictions; they are the basis for the indicator set.
5. **Define Key Intelligence Topics (KITs).** Each KIT is a standing question the program will continuously answer. A KIT has: a title, the strategic concern it addresses, the scope, the indicators that signal something is happening, the stakeholder who consumes it, and the cadence. KIT templates are in `references/templates.md`.

The output of this step is a documented **KIT register** and a **scenario set** with associated indicators. This is the spine of the program. Without it, the rest is noise.

### Step 2: Build the monitoring system

Now you can set up infrastructure. Three components:

#### 2A. Indicators and tripwires

For each KIT and each scenario, define the observable indicators that would signal it is emerging. Then decide which indicators are **tripwires**: predefined thresholds that, when triggered, escalate to a defined action. Examples:

* Indicator: "Competitor X's hiring of [specific role type] in [region]." Tripwire: "More than 5 such hires in a quarter."
* Indicator: "Regulatory filings related to [policy area]." Tripwire: "Draft regulation entering comment period."
* Indicator: "Adoption rate of [substitute technology]." Tripwire: "Crosses 10% of category."

Tripwires are what convert passive monitoring into active alerting. A monitoring system without tripwires produces dashboards no one reads.

#### 2B. Sources and collection routines

For each indicator, identify the source(s) where it can be observed, and the cadence at which the source must be checked. Routine sources include:

* Trade publications (3 to 5 core titles, plus one rotating "adjacent industry" subscription per Sharp's recommendation)
* Google Alerts or equivalent on company names, technology terms, regulatory keywords, executive names
* SEC and other regulatory filings (10-K, 10-Q, 8-K, S-1, patent filings) with automated notification where possible
* Industry analyst feeds (Gartner, IDC, Forrester, sector-specific houses)
* Executive presentations and earnings calls (transcripts are usually free within 24 hours)
* Job postings on competitor career pages
* Conference attendance and post-conference debriefs
* Customer-facing teams (sales, support, success) as standing internal sources

The source catalogue in `references/sources.md` covers the full landscape and the relative value of each category.

Configure automation where possible (alerts, RSS, AI summarization), but do not confuse automation with analysis. The system collects; the analyst interprets.

#### 2C. Network

The single highest-leverage monitoring asset is a network of people who report what they see. Two networks matter:

* **Internal network.** Sales, support, product, customer success, business development. They hear things from customers, prospects, and partners constantly. Most of this is currently lost. Set up a low-friction way to report it (a dedicated Slack channel, a Teams form, a simple email alias). What does not work: relying on people to "send it in if they remember." What works: a designated person (the analyst) who is known to handle these contributions and who reports back what was useful. Recognition, not money, is the durable incentive.
* **External network.** Industry analysts, academics, freelance journalists, specialist consultants, former employees, retired executives in the space. Build this slowly. One coffee a month with a smart outsider is worth more than a dozen subscriptions.

### Step 3: Manage the cadence and delivery

The program has two reporting modes, both required:

* **Regular briefings.** Quarterly, at minimum. Tied to executive retreats, strategy reviews, or board meetings. Format: short, visual, focused on what changed and what to watch.
* **Irregular alerts.** Fired when a tripwire trips or when convergent signals indicate a scenario is emerging. Gilad's Management Alert format is a useful template: never more than four pages; color-coded (yellow/orange/red) by severity; opens with the headline finding; includes time horizon, the evidence, the analytical interpretation, the strategic options with pros and cons, and the recommended action. A worked alert template is in `references/templates.md`.

**Critical rule (Gilad): never just send data.** Every deliverable contains interpretation. "Competitor X hired 12 sales reps in the Midwest in Q2" is data. "Competitor X has hired 12 sales reps in the Midwest in Q2, which combined with [filing] and [exec statement], indicates a regional push beginning in Q4. Our exposure is [X]. Options are [A], [B], [C]" is intelligence. The first wastes the executive's time. The second can change a decision.

### Step 4: Maintain the system

Monitoring programs degrade. Three things kill them:

* **KIT register staleness.** Strategic priorities shift; the KIT register must shift with them. Review quarterly. Retire KITs that are no longer relevant. Add new ones.
* **Alert fatigue.** If alerts go out for things that turn out not to matter, executives stop reading them. Be ruthless about the threshold for firing an alert.
* **Loss of sponsor.** When the executive who sponsored the program leaves, the program often dies. Rebuild sponsorship deliberately when leadership changes.

Run an annual review: how many alerts fired, how many were accurate, what surprised the organization that the system missed (the painful question), and what should change.

## Universal rules

These apply to both modes.

### The defensibility checklist

Before any deliverable goes to the boss, walk through these seven questions:

1. **Methodology.** Can I explain why I used the sources and methods I used?
2. **Evidence sufficiency.** Are the consequential findings backed by enough evidence? Are single-source findings flagged?
3. **Reasoning transparency.** Can someone follow the chain from evidence to conclusion?
4. **Assumptions tested.** Have I named the key assumptions and checked whether each one holds?
5. **Alternatives considered.** Did I deliberately try to construct a different interpretation of the same evidence?
6. **Confidence calibrated.** Does the language convey appropriate certainty, neither overstating nor over-hedging?
7. **Withstands scrutiny.** Would a skeptical but fair-minded reviewer find this credible?

A "yes" to all seven is the standard. A "no" anywhere is a flag to fix before delivery.

### Data, information, intelligence

These are not the same thing. The boss is paying for intelligence.

* **Data:** a raw fact (a revenue number, a job posting).
* **Information:** organized data (revenue trend over four quarters; recent hiring in a function).
* **Intelligence:** information analyzed and interpreted in light of a specific decision (the hiring pattern, combined with the patent filing and the regulatory shift, suggests a market entry move within 6 to 9 months; our exposure is X; we should consider Y).

If the deliverable stops at data or information, the work is incomplete.

### Ethical guardrails (non-negotiable)

CI is not espionage. The following are absolute prohibitions, not "be careful" suggestions:

* No misrepresentation of identity or purpose when speaking with sources.
* No theft, hacking, or unauthorized access (physical or digital).
* No bribery or inducement.
* No violation of confidentiality agreements.
* No use of information obtained through any of the above.

The grey areas (when does a conversation become elicitation? how much context must be given to a source?) require judgment. The rule of thumb: would you be comfortable explaining the method in writing to the source's general counsel? If no, do not do it.

Local laws apply on top of these (PIPEDA in Canada, GDPR in the EU, sector-specific regulation in finance, healthcare, and defense). When in doubt, check.

### What to never do

* **Skip scoping.** The most common failure.
* **Lead with data.** The second most common failure.
* **Boil the ocean.** Collecting "everything on Topic X" is not a project. It is procrastination.
* **Fixate on named competitors.** The decisive change usually comes from customers, substitutes, regulation, or adjacent industries.
* **Confuse activity with output.** Lots of reports is not the same as lots of impact. Measure the program by decisions changed, not deliverables produced.
* **Promise certainty.** CI deals in calibrated judgment under uncertainty. Pretending otherwise destroys credibility on the first miss.

## Tooling notes

### Primary path: Claude in Cowork (or Claude Code)

If the user is working with Claude in Cowork (or Claude Code) with web search and file tools available:

* Use **WebSearch** for current information. Web search is the workhorse for news, recent filings, executive statements, conference content, and most OSINT.
* Use **WebFetch** to pull specific pages (analyst posts, blog content, regulatory filings, corporate press releases). When a page returns boilerplate or "enable JavaScript," the page is client-rendered; fall back to a browser tool if available.
* Use **file tools** (Read, Write) to maintain the running source log and to draft the deliverable in the user's workspace folder.
* Use connectors if available (Box, Drive, Gmail, calendar tools) to pull internal documents the user already has.
* For ongoing monitoring, **scheduled tasks** can run a recurring search-and-summarize loop and notify the user on a cadence. Pair with **artifacts** to create a live, refreshable dashboard the user (or their boss) can revisit. This is a sweet spot for Cowork.
* For document creation, use the appropriate skill (docx, pptx, xlsx, pdf) to produce the deliverable in the requested format. Decide format in the scoping conversation, not at the end.

### Alternate path: any AI assistant plus a browser

If the user is working with ChatGPT, Claude.ai web, Gemini, or another assistant without integrated tools:

* Manual search and copy/paste is slower but the methodology is identical. Maintain the source log in a separate document.
* The AI's job is to help with: scoping questions, framework application (Five Forces, BIM, scenario building), synthesis across many inputs, deliverable drafting, and pre-mortem stress-testing of conclusions.
* Be more skeptical of any factual claim the AI surfaces from training data. Always verify against a primary source.
* For monitoring, the user will need to assemble the infrastructure separately (Google Alerts, RSS readers, SEC filing alerts, a notes system). The AI can summarize and synthesize on a cadence the user runs manually.

### A note on AI-generated content

When using any AI to help with CI work, two failure modes are common: (1) the AI invents facts that sound plausible, and (2) the AI presents synthesized information as if it were a primary finding. Both are killers in CI work. Treat AI output as a first draft to be verified, not as a source. Cite primary sources in the deliverable, not the AI.

## Quick mode selector

Use this to decide which workflow to run.

| If the user says… | Use mode |
|---|---|
| "My boss wants a brief on…" | A (Snapshot) |
| "We're considering [decision] and need a read on the market" | A (Snapshot) |
| "Profile [competitor]" | A (Snapshot), possibly graduating to B |
| "We need to start tracking…" | B (Monitoring) |
| "Set up alerts on…" | B (Monitoring) |
| "I want to know if [competitor] does X" | B (Monitoring) with tripwires |
| "Build us a competitive early warning system" | B (Monitoring), full CEW |
| "Both, ideally" | A first, then B, using A to define B's scope |
