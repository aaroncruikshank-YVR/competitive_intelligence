# CI analytical frameworks

The frameworks that turn collected information into intelligence. Use at least two in any meaningful project. Apply them deliberately, not as decoration. Each one answers a different question; the choice depends on the intelligence question being asked.

---

## Porter's Five Forces

**Question it answers:** What forces shape competitive intensity and structural profitability in this industry?

**Why it matters:** Profitability is structural. Strong forces compress profit; weak forces protect it. Understanding the forces tells you where competitive pressure will come from and which moves will improve or worsen the structure.

**The five forces, with prompts for each:**

1. **Rivalry among existing competitors.**
   * How many competitors are there, and how concentrated is the market?
   * What is the rate of industry growth? Slow growth intensifies rivalry.
   * Are products differentiated, or commodified?
   * What are the exit barriers? High exit barriers keep weak players in and intensify rivalry.
   * What is the diversity of competitors (strategic, cultural, national)?

2. **Threat of new entrants.**
   * What are the barriers to entry? Capital, brand, switching costs, scale economies, regulation, network effects, distribution access?
   * Are there recent or imminent entrants? Who and from where?
   * How would incumbents respond to entry?

3. **Bargaining power of buyers.**
   * How concentrated are the buyers?
   * What is their switching cost?
   * How price-sensitive are they?
   * Is the product differentiated enough to reduce buyer power?
   * Can buyers integrate backward and become competitors?

4. **Bargaining power of suppliers.**
   * How concentrated is the supply base?
   * Are there substitutes for the supplier's input?
   * How important is this industry to the supplier's volume?
   * Can suppliers integrate forward?

5. **Threat of substitutes.**
   * What other ways can the customer get the job done?
   * What is the price-performance trade-off of substitutes?
   * What is the trajectory of substitute improvement?
   * What is the switching cost?

**How to use it in a snapshot:**
* Spend 30 minutes per force. Rate each on a 1 to 5 scale.
* Identify the two or three most powerful forces. These are where the strategic action is.
* For each high-force area, identify the trend (is it strengthening or weakening?). Trend matters more than current state.

**Common mistake:** Treating Five Forces as a static diagram. The structural question is "where is this going?" not "where is this now?"

## PESTEL

**Question it answers:** What is happening in the macro environment that could change the competitive landscape?

**The six categories, with example prompts:**

* **Political:** Government stability, trade policy, regulatory direction, policy shifts.
* **Economic:** Growth, inflation, interest rates, exchange rates, employment, capital availability.
* **Social:** Demographics, cultural shifts, attitudes, lifestyle changes, generational dynamics.
* **Technological:** Emerging technologies, R&D trajectories, adoption curves, infrastructure shifts.
* **Environmental:** Climate-related risk, resource availability, sustainability pressure, ESG dynamics.
* **Legal:** Specific legislation, litigation trends, IP regimes, employment law, antitrust.

**How to use it:** Quick scan, two to four bullets per category. Then pull the threads: any PESTEL factor that materially affects one of the Five Forces is a priority for deeper analysis. PESTEL alone produces a list; PESTEL plus Five Forces produces an interpretation.

## Strategic group mapping

**Question it answers:** Which competitors are actually competing with us? Which are different animals?

**Process:**

1. Pick two axes that capture meaningful strategic variation in the industry (examples: breadth of product line vs. geographic scope; premium vs. mass; integrated vs. specialist; direct vs. channel; technology-led vs. service-led).
2. Plot every meaningful competitor on the grid.
3. Identify the clusters (strategic groups).
4. Assess **mobility barriers** between groups. What would have to change for a member of one group to compete in another? High mobility barriers mean groups are effectively separate sub-markets.

**Why it matters:** Most "competitor lists" lump everyone together. Strategic groups reveal which competitors actually constrain each other and which compete in different markets entirely. The user's "main competitor" may be in a different strategic group, with different economics and customers.

**Common mistake:** Picking axes that are obvious (size, age) rather than strategic. Axes should reflect choices that drive different economics.

## Porter's four-component competitor profile

**Question it answers:** How will this competitor behave? What will they do next?

**The four components:**

1. **Future goals.** What are they trying to achieve? Growth? Profit? Market share? Survival? Look at what they say (stated goals), what they spend on (actual priorities), and what their incentive structures reward (compensation, board pressure).
2. **Current strategy.** What is their actual current play, in market terms? How are they competing? Where? With what?
3. **Assumptions.** What do they believe about themselves, the market, their customers, the competitive landscape? These are usually unstated and often wrong. Inferring them is a core CI skill.
4. **Capabilities.** What can they actually do? Strengths and weaknesses across financial, technological, operational, brand, and human capital dimensions.

**Why all four matter:** A competitor's behavior is driven by all four together. They will pursue their goals through their current strategy as long as their assumptions hold and within the limits of their capabilities. Predicting their next move requires reading all four.

**Sources for each:**
* Goals: annual reports, exec speeches, compensation structure, investor commitments.
* Strategy: actual market behavior, allocation of resources, recent moves.
* Assumptions: what they have stopped doing, what they refuse to do, their stated worldview, their reaction to past events.
* Capabilities: financials, headcount, technology assets, recent investments, patents, talent profile.

## BIM: Blindspots Identification Methodology (Gilad)

**Question it answers:** What is this competitor (or our own organization) wrong about that we can exploit?

**Four-step process:**

1. **Inventory the target's assumptions.** Drawing on the competitor profile above, list what they believe about: buyer preferences, the basis of competition, barriers to entry, supply chain dynamics, substitutes, their own strengths.
2. **Re-analyze the same forces *without regard to* those assumptions.** Apply Five Forces, customer research, market data, etc., as if you knew none of the target's beliefs. What does the evidence say?
3. **Compare.** Where do the target's beliefs and the evidence diverge? Each divergence is a potential blindspot.
4. **Prioritize.** Blindspots matter only if they are strategically consequential. Rank by: how confident is the target in this belief? how wrong is the belief? what would it cost them to act on the wrong belief? what could a competitor (you) do to exploit it?

**Use it on yourself.** The most valuable BIM exercise is often on the user's own organization. The questions become: what are our leadership's assumptions? Are they still true? What is the cost of being wrong?

**Why it matters:** Industry change punishes the incumbent whose assumptions stop being true and who does not notice. Gilad's central claim: most strategic failure traces to "obsolete internal convictions." BIM is the antidote.

## Scenario planning (for CI, simplified)

**Question it answers:** Under what futures does our strategy still work? Under what futures does it break?

**Four-step process:**

1. **Identify the key uncertainties.** What two or three external variables will most shape the future of this market, *and* are most uncertain?
2. **Pick two as the axes.** Each should be bipolar (e.g., regulation tight vs. loose; technology adoption fast vs. slow).
3. **Build the 2×2.** Four quadrants, each a coherent future scenario. Name each one (memorable names help adoption).
4. **For each scenario:**
   * Describe what the world looks like (2 to 3 paragraphs).
   * Identify the leading indicators that would suggest this scenario is emerging.
   * Assess the implication for the organization's current strategy. Where is it strong? Where does it break?
   * Identify "no regret" moves that work across all four.

**Gilad's framing:** "Imagination, not computing power, is the true limiting factor." Do not spend money on simulation software. Spend time on the question of what could plausibly change.

**Use the worst-case scenario explicitly.** It is the prod that forces executive attention and the basis for tripwires in the monitoring program.

## Christensen's disruption lens

**Question it answers:** Is the industry vulnerable to disruption from below, and if so, by whom?

**The disruption pattern (simplified):**

* Incumbents serve mainstream customers with feature-rich, high-margin products.
* A new entrant targets the low end, or a non-consumer, with a "good enough" product that is cheaper, simpler, or more accessible.
* Incumbents ignore the entrant because the entrant's customers are unattractive (low margin).
* The entrant improves over time, eventually serving the mainstream customer at a lower cost.
* By the time incumbents respond, the entrant has structural cost advantages and the incumbent's business model cannot match it.

**Diagnostic questions:**

* Who is currently *not* using this product because it is too expensive, too complex, or too feature-rich for their need?
* Is anyone serving them with a simpler, cheaper alternative?
* Is the trajectory of the alternative pointing toward the mainstream?
* What does the incumbent's response look like? Are they ignoring it? Dismissing it? (Both are bad signs for the incumbent.)

**When the lens applies:** When there is a non-consuming customer segment or a customer segment that overshoots on features. When a new entrant has a structurally different cost model. When the alternative is improving on a trajectory that points toward the mainstream.

**When it does not apply:** Not all new competition is "disruption." A direct, head-on competitor doing the same thing better is not disruption. It is competition. Use the lens carefully.

## KIT register (Gilad / MIBOK)

**Question it answers:** What standing intelligence does this organization need, on what cadence, for whom?

**For each KIT, document:**

| Field | Example |
|---|---|
| Title | "Competitor X market entry signals" |
| Strategic concern | "X's entry would compress our pricing in core segment" |
| Scope | "X's hiring, product moves, partnerships in [region], 12-month horizon" |
| Sub-questions | "Are they staffing a sales presence? Are they filing for regulatory approval? Are they signing channel partners?" |
| Indicators | "Sales hires in region; regulatory filings; partnership announcements; product certifications" |
| Tripwires | ">5 sales hires/quarter; any regulatory filing; announced partner of >$50M scale" |
| Stakeholder | "CMO and head of strategy" |
| Cadence | "Weekly scan, quarterly briefing, alert on tripwire" |
| Priority | "P1 of 3 tiers" |
| Review date | "Quarterly" |

**Discipline:** Review the register quarterly. Retire KITs that are no longer relevant. Add new ones as strategy shifts. A stale KIT register is the leading indicator of a dying monitoring program.

## Sharp's gap mapping (four quadrants)

**Question it answers:** What do we know, what do we think we know, and where is the dangerous gap?

**Sort organizational knowledge on a topic into:**

1. **Validated and current.** Information independently verified within the relevant timeframe. The smallest quadrant in most organizations.
2. **Believed but not verified.** Assumptions everyone operates on but no one has tested. Usually the most dangerous.
3. **Out of date.** Once true; no one has checked recently.
4. **Genuine unknowns.** Acknowledged blind spots.

**Process:**
* In a workshop or solo exercise, list 10 to 20 statements that members of the organization treat as true about the topic.
* Classify each into a quadrant. Be honest.
* Prioritize the quadrant 2 items by consequence. These are the highest-leverage CI questions.
* Validate or refute through the project.

## Pre-mortem

**Question it answers:** What is wrong with our recommended decision that we haven't seen?

**Process:**

1. State the recommendation as if it has been made.
2. State (counterfactually) that 12 months later, the decision has failed badly.
3. Each participant writes the story of how the failure happened. 10 minutes.
4. Surface the failure modes. Anything that surfaces is a gap in your analysis. Address it.

**Why it works:** "What could go wrong?" elicits caution and vague responses. "Imagine it *did* go wrong, and write the story" elicits specific failure modes and concrete weak points. The framing matters.

## Devil's advocacy and ACH (Analysis of Competing Hypotheses)

For consequential analytical conclusions:

* **Devil's advocacy:** Assign someone (or yourself in a different sitting) to construct the strongest counter-argument to your conclusion. If you cannot construct a credible counter-argument, you may not have looked hard enough.
* **ACH:** For each piece of evidence, evaluate which hypotheses it supports, contradicts, or is neutral on. The hypothesis with the least *contradicting* evidence wins (not the one with the most *supporting* evidence). This protects against confirmation bias.

## Framework selection cheat sheet

| Question you are trying to answer | Frameworks to use |
|---|---|
| Why is this industry as profitable as it is? | Five Forces, value chain |
| How are competitors clustered? | Strategic group map |
| How will this specific competitor behave? | Porter's four-component profile + BIM |
| What futures should we plan for? | Scenario planning |
| Who could disrupt us? | Christensen disruption lens + adjacency scan |
| What do we wrongly believe? | BIM (applied to self) + Sharp's gap mapping |
| What macro shifts matter? | PESTEL, then map to Five Forces |
| Are we missing something obvious? | Pre-mortem, devil's advocacy, ACH |
| What should we monitor continuously? | KIT register + scenario-derived indicators |
