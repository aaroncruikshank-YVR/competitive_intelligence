# CI deliverable templates

Working templates for the artifacts a competitive intelligence project produces. Adapt formatting to the user's organizational norms; the underlying structure is what matters.

---

## 1. Stakeholder intake protocol

Use this in the scoping conversation with the requester (the boss). 30 to 45 minutes. Take notes verbatim where possible; the requester's exact words often reveal more than their stated request.

**Section A: The decision**

1. What decision will this work inform? Be specific.
2. What will you do differently depending on what we find?
3. What is the timeline for the decision?
4. Who else is involved? Who needs to be convinced, and what would convince them?
5. What is the cost of getting this wrong?

**Section B: The current mental model**

6. What do you already believe to be true about this market/competitor/topic?
7. Where did those beliefs come from? When were they last checked?
8. What would surprise you?
9. What would force you to change your strategy?
10. What is your worst case?

**Section C: Signals you have already noticed**

11. What have you heard recently from a salesperson, customer, partner, or supplier that you didn't fully understand or that surprised you?
12. What are you most worried about?
13. What "everyone knows" about this market that you are skeptical of?

**Section D: Scope and deliverable**

14. What questions, specifically, do you want answered?
15. What is in scope, and what is out?
16. What format do you want? (One-pager? Slide deck? Memo? Briefing?)
17. How long? When do you need it?
18. Who else will read it?

**Output: a one-paragraph scope contract**

After the conversation, draft:

> "We will produce [deliverable type] addressing [3 to 7 intelligence questions] for [stakeholder], by [date], to inform the decision about [decision]. Scope includes [in-scope]; explicitly out of scope: [out-of-scope]. We will use [primary sources/methods] and target [confidence level] on the core findings."

Send to the requester. Get explicit confirmation. **Do not start collecting until they agree.**

## 2. Competitive snapshot brief (the deliverable for Mode A)

The default structure for a written CI brief. Adjust length to audience: 1 page for a C-suite quick read, 5 to 15 pages for a strategy team. Resist the urge to inflate.

```
[TITLE] Competitive Intelligence Brief: [Topic]
Prepared for: [Stakeholder]
Prepared by: [Analyst]
Date: [Date]
Confidence: [Overall confidence level with definition]

================================
EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (1 page)
================================
- Decision being supported: [restate]
- Top 3 to 5 findings (with confidence per finding)
- Recommended actions or considerations
- Key risks and what we could not determine

================================
BACKGROUND AND SCOPE (0.5 to 1 page)
================================
- The decision context
- The intelligence questions addressed
- Methodology summary
- What was in scope and out
- Limitations

================================
FINDINGS (3 to 8 pages)
================================

Finding 1: [Headline statement]
- Confidence: [High / Moderate / Low]
- Evidence summary (with sources cited)
- Interpretation
- So what: [implication for the decision]

Finding 2: [Headline statement]
[same structure]

[etc.]

================================
RECOMMENDATIONS / CONSIDERATIONS (0.5 to 1 page)
================================
- What we recommend, framed as options where appropriate
- Pros and cons of each
- Trigger conditions for revisiting

================================
WHAT WE COULD NOT DETERMINE (0.5 page)
================================
- Explicit list of questions we could not answer
- Why (source limitations, time, access)
- What would be required to close each gap

================================
APPENDIX
================================
- Source log (all sources consulted, with date accessed)
- Competitor profile detail (if applicable)
- Methodology notes
- Glossary if specialized terms
```

**Confidence vocabulary (define at the top of the brief):**

* **High:** Based on multiple independent reliable sources with strong corroboration. Defensible against challenge.
* **Moderate:** Based on partial evidence with some corroboration but with one or more notable limitations. The conclusion is the most likely interpretation of the available evidence.
* **Low:** Based on limited evidence, single source, or significant inference. Directional only.
* **No confidence:** We do not have a basis to answer this question. Listed for transparency.

## 3. One-page executive summary (for time-poor executives)

When the audience reads one page and only one page. The body of the brief becomes the appendix.

```
[Topic] – Competitive Intelligence One-Pager
For: [Executive] | From: [Analyst] | [Date]

THE QUESTION
[One sentence: the decision or strategic question this addresses.]

THE ANSWER
[Two to four sentences: the bottom-line finding. Lead with conclusion.]

KEY EVIDENCE
- [Finding 1 + confidence + 1-line source]
- [Finding 2 + confidence + 1-line source]
- [Finding 3 + confidence + 1-line source]

IMPLICATIONS
- [What this means for the decision: 2 to 4 bullets]

WHAT TO WATCH
- [Indicators or developments that would change the assessment]

CAVEATS
- [What we could not verify; key assumptions]

[Full brief and source log attached / available on request.]
```

## 4. Competitor profile template

Use this when the assignment is a deep read on a specific company.

```
COMPETITOR PROFILE: [Company Name]
[Date] | Confidence: [Overall level]

1. SNAPSHOT
- Headquarters, founded, ownership structure
- Revenue (latest, with source and date)
- Headcount (latest, with source)
- Geographic presence
- Core products / services

2. LEADERSHIP AND OWNERSHIP
- CEO and key executives (tenure, backgrounds, signals from their public commentary)
- Board composition
- Major shareholders or owners (and their interests)
- Recent executive changes (and what they signal)

3. PORTER FOUR-COMPONENT PROFILE
   3a. Goals (stated and inferred)
   3b. Current strategy
   3c. Assumptions (especially those that might be wrong)
   3d. Capabilities (strengths and weaknesses)

4. RECENT MOVES
- Product launches, geographic moves, M&A, partnerships, executive hires
- 18- to 24-month timeline

5. STRATEGIC TRAJECTORY
- Where do all signals point them next?
- What scenarios for their next 12 to 24 months are most plausible?

6. BLINDSPOTS (apply BIM)
- Assumptions they appear to hold
- Where the evidence diverges from their assumptions
- Strategic vulnerabilities

7. IMPLICATIONS FOR US
- Threats
- Opportunities (what their blindspots create for us)
- Recommended actions or watch areas

8. WHAT WE COULD NOT DETERMINE

9. SOURCES
```

## 5. Management alert (for Mode B monitoring)

The format for a fired alert in an ongoing monitoring program. Adapted from Gilad's Management Alert format. **Never more than 4 pages.** Color-coded by severity.

```
================================
MANAGEMENT ALERT [Yellow / Orange / Red]
================================
Subject: [Headline finding in one line]
For: [Stakeholder]
From: [Analyst / CI function]
Date: [Date]
KIT reference: [KIT title from register]

THE FINDING (1 paragraph)
[What happened, or what converging signals indicate is happening.]

WHY THIS MATTERS NOW
[Why this triggers an alert rather than a routine entry in the next briefing.
What tripwire (if any) was hit.]

EVIDENCE
- [Signal 1: source, date, what it shows, confidence]
- [Signal 2: ...]
- [Signal 3: ...]
- Convergence: [why these signals together support the interpretation]

ANALYSIS
[2 to 4 paragraphs: what the analyst concludes is happening, why, and what
alternative interpretations were considered and why they are less likely.]

TIME HORIZON
[When this matters by. Is this a 30-day window? 6 months? Longer?]

STRATEGIC OPTIONS
- Option A: [description, pros, cons, expected competitor response]
- Option B: [description, pros, cons, expected competitor response]
- Option C: [description, pros, cons, expected competitor response]

RECOMMENDATION
[The analyst's recommended option with brief rationale. Be explicit.]

WHAT TO WATCH NEXT
[2 to 4 specific indicators that would confirm or refute this read.]
```

**Severity codes:**
* **Yellow:** Notable development worth attention. No immediate action required.
* **Orange:** Material development. Strategic options should be reviewed.
* **Red:** Critical. Strategic response decision required in defined timeframe.

## 6. Quarterly briefing template (for Mode B regular cadence)

The regular rhythm deliverable. Pairs with the irregular alerts.

```
QUARTERLY COMPETITIVE BRIEFING – Q[X] [Year]
For: [Stakeholder / Leadership team]

================================
WHAT CHANGED THIS QUARTER (2 to 3 pages)
================================
Top 5 to 7 developments across the monitored landscape.
For each: what happened, what it means, what we are doing about it.

================================
KIT BY KIT STATUS (1 to 2 pages)
================================
Brief status per KIT:
- KIT 1: [Status: stable / moving / hot] [1-paragraph update]
- KIT 2: ...
- etc.

================================
SCENARIO TRACKING (1 page)
================================
For each of our scenarios (from Step 1 of monitoring setup):
- Which indicators have lit up this quarter?
- Which scenario is gaining or losing plausibility?

================================
ALERTS FIRED THIS QUARTER
================================
Recap of any alerts issued. Brief summary; full alert texts in appendix.

================================
WHAT TO WATCH NEXT QUARTER
================================
Forward-looking: what we will be paying particular attention to and why.

================================
PROGRAM HOUSEKEEPING
================================
- KIT register changes (added / retired)
- Source additions or drops
- Methodology notes

================================
APPENDIX
- Alert texts
- Source log
- Quarterly stats (signals reviewed, alerts fired, etc.)
```

## 7. Source log (working document for any project)

Maintain throughout the project. The traceability matters for defensibility.

| Date | Source | Type | What I found | Implication | Confidence | Corroborated by |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2026-02-12 | Acme Corp 10-K p.18 | Reg filing | Reports 32% YoY EU growth | Confirms EU expansion priority | High | TechCrunch story 2026-01-30 |
| 2026-02-14 | LinkedIn search "Acme + sales director + EMEA" | Job/people | 7 new sales director hires in EMEA Q4-Q1 | Reinforces EU push; signals scale of investment | Moderate | Above |

A spreadsheet works. A Notion or Airtable database works. What matters is that any consequential finding can be traced back to the underlying evidence in minutes.

## 9. Monitoring cadence pattern

A typical week and a typical quarter for an in-house CI analyst running an active monitoring program.

**Weekly:**
* Monday: scan automated alert digests (news, regulatory, social) from the previous week. Triage into "noise / log / develop further."
* Wednesday: 30-minute call with a rotating internal source (sales, support, product, BD) to catch what they have heard.
* Thursday: scan executive social posts, recent earnings calls in the sector, conference content.
* Friday: 1-hour analysis block. Updates to source log. Decide whether any signals warrant an alert.

**Monthly:**
* One half-day deep dive on a specific KIT.
* One outside-the-industry trade publication scan (Sharp's adjacency rotation).
* One 30-minute external expert conversation.

**Quarterly:**
* Produce the quarterly briefing.
* Review the KIT register.
* Review alerts fired vs. accuracy.
* Review what surprised the organization that the system missed.
* Update the scenario set if the environment has shifted meaningfully.

**Annually:**
* Program review against original objectives.
* Sponsor re-engagement.
* Decide whether to add, remove, or restructure KITs.

## 10. Pre-publish checklist (the last 30 minutes before delivery)

Before any CI deliverable goes to the requester, walk through:

1. Is the answer to the question on page 1 or slide 1?
2. Are confidence levels explicit?
3. Are sources cited and reachable?
4. Did I run the pre-mortem on the recommendation?
5. Did I look for disconfirming evidence?
6. Did I name what I could not determine?
7. Would I be comfortable defending this in a 60-minute meeting?
8. Did I match the format to what the requester asked for?
9. Did anyone with domain knowledge review it?
10. Is there anywhere I am presenting opinion as fact?

A "no" anywhere means stop and fix before sending.
